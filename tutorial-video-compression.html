Compression

What compression actually does

Compression reduces the number of bits needed to store or transmit video. The key idea is to remove redundancy: repeated values, predictable structure, or detail that can be approximated without changing the interpretation of the video.

At a high level, uncompressed video stores every frame directly. Lossless methods reorganize the data so it can be reconstructed exactly. Lossy methods allow controlled error so the file becomes much smaller, trading size for fidelity.

In practice, most workflows mix several ideas: entropy coding, spatial transforms, temporal prediction, and quantization. The correct choice depends on the use case. Archival storage and quantitative analysis usually prioritize fidelity, while sharing, review, and long-term storage often require stronger compression.

Major approaches

Compression families used in this comparison

Raw / uncompressed

Every frame is stored directly. This preserves everything, but storage costs scale immediately with frame count and bit depth.

Lossless codecs

FFV1 and x265 lossless reconstruct the exact original frames. They reduce redundancy without changing pixel values.

Intra-frame lossy

MJPEG compresses each frame independently. It is simple and fast, but usually less storage-efficient than predictive codecs.

Predictive lossy

x265 uses spatial and temporal prediction. CRF settings increase compression by allowing progressively larger approximation error.
